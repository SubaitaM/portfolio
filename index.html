Does Recession Music Exist?

January 2024

The Question

When the economy crashes, does our music change? I wanted to know if recessions leave an audible fingerprint on popular music.

The Data

I analyzed 50 years of Billboard Hot 100 data, combining it with:

  • Economic indicators (GDP growth, unemployment rates)
  • Spotify audio features (valence, energy, tempo)
  • Lyric sentiment analysis

Key Findings

1. Songs get slower during recessions
Average tempo dropped by 8-12 BPM during major downturns (1982, 2008).

2. Lyrical nostalgia increases
References to "old times," "remember," and past decades spiked 40% during recession periods.

3. But not necessarily sadder
Surprisingly, valence (musical positivity) remained stable. Songs were slower and more reflective, but not more negative.

The Surprising Twist

The strongest correlation wasn't with recession timing—it was with recovery. The most upbeat, energetic music appeared 6-12 months after unemployment peaked, suggesting music reflects hope for recovery more than current hardship.

Methods

Python analysis using pandas, spotipy API, VADER sentiment analysis, and statsmodels for time series regression.

Universal Blue: Color Emotions Across Cultures

December 2023

The Question

Is blue universally perceived as sad? Do Japanese speakers associate colors with emotions differently than Spanish speakers?

The Data

I analyzed survey data from 30 countries (n=4,598) where participants rated how strongly they associated 12 colors with 20 emotions.

Universal Patterns

Red = Anger/Love: 96% cross-cultural agreement
Yellow = Happy: 89% agreement
Black = Sad/Fear: 87% agreement

The Blue Surprise

Blue showed the most cultural variation. While 71% of Western respondents associated blue with sadness, only 34% of East Asian respondents did. Instead, blue was strongly associated with "calm" and "trust."

Cultural Clusters

K-means clustering revealed three distinct color-emotion patterns:

  • Western cluster: Blue=sad, purple=mysterious
  • East Asian cluster: Blue=calm, white=death
  • Latin American cluster: Stronger warm color emotions

Methods

Statistical analysis using scikit-learn, hierarchical clustering, and cultural dimension correlation analysis.
